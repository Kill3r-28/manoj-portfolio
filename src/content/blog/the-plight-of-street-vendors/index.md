# The plight of street vendors

Last Sunday I was on a walk in the neighborhood and the road was blocked because of a quarrel between two street vendors. They both were selling apples on cart. Quarrel was about the place of sale. One of them was arguing that the place was his as he has been selling there for the past two years. The other guy didn’t buy that, he argued that the road is public property and it is not any single person’s, the rage went on. While all this was happening the entire road was blocked and traffic was jammed creating nuisance. Police came to rescue and ultimately both of them were evicted from there.

That incident got me thinking about what rights does one hold regarding use of public property like roads does. More importantly, using roads for selling goods and services and making profit. This is serious, because both the sellers are made to vacate on grounds of nuisance although they were living through honest means. One must remember that **80% of the India’s working population works in informal sector** (self employed or employment in unregistered micro enterprises). Isn’t it the state’s responsibility to find a way around and pass regulations and directives?

So I found that government of India has passed a **"Streets Vendors Act:2014"** which addressed this issue and it took 70 long years for GoI to realise the plight of street vendors and legitimately save them from predatory state actors like police and municipal authorities.

**Postulates of the act are:**

1. **Protection against eviction:** The municipal bodies in all cities are bound to count the number of vendors in the city and provide them a certificate of approval after charging them a nominal fee. And no eviction should take place before the state’s counting and declaring that a specific vendor is not in compliance with rules. 30 days of notice period must be given to the vendor in case of eviction request after getting certified.

2. **Formation of committees:** The act asks for formation of **TVC (town vending committees)** comprising 40% vendors and 10% from Non-governmental organisations to tackle issues like harassment from police or any other law enforcement bodies.

3. **Enumeration and allocation:** It clearly states that there can be no cap as to how many vendors can operate in location. If the maximum capacity is reached in a locality, vendors need to be located to neighboring locations until arrangements are made and they can return to the locality.

The above are basic inferences I could make out of the act. It also deals with the specific procedures and duties of municipal and local bodies.

Awareness among vendors and pressure on local government bodies would help in providing the street vendors what legitimately belongs to them. **Center for Civil Society** is one such NGO which fights for the vendors rights. I will leave a link to check their work.

Meanwhile I would strongly recommend everybody to support the local street vendors.

**Jai Hind!**
